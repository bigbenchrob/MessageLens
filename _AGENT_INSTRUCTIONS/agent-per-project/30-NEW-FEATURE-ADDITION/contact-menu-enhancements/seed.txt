Yeah, this is a classic “too many items in the sidebar” problem. There are well-worn patterns for it, and none of them require the keyboard, even if they allow it.

Here are a few that would translate well to your macOS Flutter app:

⸻

1. Alphabetical grouping with collapsible sections

Instead of one giant flat list:
	•	Group contacts under section headers: A, B, C…
	•	Make each section collapsible:
	•	Clicking the header toggles that block.
	•	Optionally show a small count badge on the header: “B (23)”.
	•	Keep the currently open section sticky at the top as you scroll.

Mouse-only workflow:
	•	User scrolls a bit, sees the section they want, clicks header if needed, and clicks the contact. No keyboard required.
	•	Keyboard users can still type to jump, but it’s purely additive.

Implementation-ish in Flutter:
	•	CustomScrollView with SliverPersistentHeader per letter, or a package like sticky_headers/grouped_list.
	•	Precompute a Map<Letter, List<Contact>>.

⸻

2. A vertical A–Z index “jump bar”

Think iOS Contacts on the right edge, but adapted to your sidebar:
	•	Add a slim vertical strip next to the list: A, B, C… Z, and maybe “★” for favourites and “#” for non-alpha.
	•	Hovering over a letter highlights it; clicking it scrolls the main list to that section.
	•	You can even support click-and-drag down the bar (like iOS) if you want to get fancy, but a simple click is already a big win.

Mouse-only workflow:
	•	User just flicks the mouse to the index, clicks S, and your ListView jumps to the S-section. Then one more click on the contact.

This plays nicely with #1: the jump bar just targets those grouped sections.

⸻

3. Favorites / “Pinned” contacts at the top

Most people talk to a small subset of their contacts a lot.

Pattern:
	•	Have a Favorites section at the very top of the sidebar (like Mail’s “Favorites” mailboxes).
	•	Let users pin/unpin contacts via a star icon or context menu.
	•	In the main list below, you can either:
	•	Keep favorites also visible (but highlighted), or
	•	Remove them from the main list to avoid duplication.

Mouse-only workflow:
	•	Daily use: click one of the first 5–10 pinned contacts.
	•	Only occasionally scroll or jump via A–Z for the long tail.

⸻

4. Smart / filtered groups (e.g. “Recents”)

For an iMessage viewer this is particularly natural:
	•	Recents: contacts you’ve opened in your app recently.
	•	Top chats: contacts with most messages, or most recent activity in a time window.
	•	System groups: “Family”, “Work”, etc. if that’s meaningful in your app.

These can live above the full contact list, same way Mail has Favorites and Smart Mailboxes in the sidebar.

Mouse-only workflow:
	•	User almost never needs to traverse the entire list; they pick from “Recents” or “Top chats” most of the time.

⸻

5. Optional search field (but not required)

I’d still include the search field you imagined:
	•	Place a search box above the contact list.
	•	As the user types, filter the entire list and dim or hide group headers.
	•	Show a small badge with the count of matching contacts.

Key point: don’t depend on it. It’s there as a power feature, but:
	•	Mouse-only users can happily ignore it.
	•	Keyboard-inclined users will love that they can hit ⌘F / ⌘L / whatever you wire up, type “ro”, hit Return, and jump.

⸻

6. Sensible defaults + progressive disclosure

If you combine:
	1.	Favorites / Recents at the top
	2.	Grouped A–Z list
	3.	A–Z jump bar on the side
	4.	Optional search

…then the flow for someone with hundreds of contacts is still simple:
	•	Most of the time: one click in Favorites or Recents.
	•	Otherwise:
	•	One click on a letter in the jump bar → list jumps.
	•	One click on the contact.

Total: two mouse clicks, no keyboard.

⸻

How I’d start in your app

If you want a minimal, mac-idiomatic first pass:
	1.	Group by first display letter with sticky headers.
	2.	Add a Favorites section above that.
	3.	Add an A–Z jump strip on the right edge of the sidebar.
	4.	Add a search field above everything but treat it as optional.

You can refine from there (e.g., recents, smart groups, badges, etc.), but even that initial combination should make “hundreds of contacts” feel manageable without ever forcing keyboard use.
